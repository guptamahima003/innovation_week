🛍️ The Big Picture
Imagine you walk into a Best Buy store. A really smart sales associate notices:

"This person always waits for sales" → They show you a coupon
"This person loves the latest gadgets" → They show you what just launched
"This person reads every review before buying" → They hand you a comparison guide
This app does exactly that — but online, automatically, and in real-time.

🧩 How It Works (Step by Step)
Step 1: We Create Fake Customers (Synthetic Data)
Since this is a hackathon demo, we don't have real Best Buy customers. So the app generates 10,000 fake customer profiles that look realistic.

Each fake customer has:

Who they are: Name, age, city, loyalty membership level
What they do online: How often they visit, how long they browse, what they search
What they've bought: Past purchases, average spending, return history
What they prefer: Favorite brands (Apple? Samsung?), are they price-sensitive or not?
Their life situation: Income range, homeowner, has kids, etc.
Think of it like creating 10,000 detailed character profiles for a movie — each one different.

Step 2: The AI Groups Customers into "Personas" (K-Means Clustering)
The app feeds all 10,000 profiles into a machine learning algorithm called K-Means. This algorithm looks at patterns and says:

"Hmm, these 2,200 customers all have high price sensitivity, love deals, and abandon their cart when prices seem too high. Let me group them together as Value Hunters."

It does this automatically and creates 6 customer types:

Persona	Who They Are	Real Example
🚀 Tech Enthusiast	Loves new gadgets, doesn't care much about price	Person who buys every new iPhone on launch day
🏷️ Value Hunter	Always looking for deals, abandons cart if price feels high	Person who waits for Black Friday to buy a TV
🔬 Considered Researcher	Reads every review, compares specs for days	Person who spends 3 weeks researching which laptop to buy
⭐ Loyalty Power User	Long-time customer, big spender, member rewards	Person who has been shopping at Best Buy for 10 years
💤 Lapsing Customer	Used to shop a lot but hasn't visited in months	Person who bought a lot last year but disappeared
💼 Business Buyer	Buys in bulk for their company	IT manager ordering 20 laptops for the office
Step 3: The Storefront (What the Customer Sees)
When you open the app, you see a Best Buy-style online store:

A blue-and-yellow navigation bar
50 real-looking products (TVs, laptops, headphones, etc.)
Product detail pages with ratings and descriptions
A shopping cart and checkout page
Behind the scenes, the app secretly assigns you one of the 6 personas. There's a small badge in the corner so you can see which persona you got (for demo purposes).

Step 4: Everything You Do Is Tracked (Event Tracking)
Every action you take on the storefront sends a signal to the backend through a WebSocket (think of it as a live, always-open phone line between your browser and the server):

Your Action	What the System Records
You look at a product	"User browsed Samsung 65" TV for 45 seconds"
You add something to cart	"User added $1,299 TV to cart"
You search for something	"User searched for 'gaming laptop'"
You start checkout	"User began checkout with $1,299 in cart"
You leave a page	"User left the cart page after 30 seconds"
Your mouse moves to close the tab	"Exit intent detected!"
Step 5: Abandon Detection (Did They Leave?)
The system watches for abandonment signals — signs that a customer is about to leave without buying:

Signal	What It Means
Added to cart → left the cart page	Cart abandon — They were interested but walked away
Started checkout → left	Checkout abandon — Something stopped them at payment
Looked at a product for 30+ seconds → left without adding to cart	Product page abandon — They were interested but not convinced
Searched for something → didn't click any result	Search abandon — They couldn't find what they wanted
Step 6: Why Did They Leave? (Reason Classification)
Here's where it gets smart. The system doesn't just know that someone left — it figures out WHY they left by combining the abandon signal with their persona:

The Signal	+ The Persona	= The Reason
Left cart with $1,299 TV	Value Hunter (price-sensitive)	Price too high — They think it costs too much
Left checkout page	Tech Enthusiast (not price-sensitive)	Payment friction — The payment process was annoying
Looked at a product for 2 minutes, then left	Researcher (reads everything)	Confidence gap — They're not sure it's the right choice
Searched but didn't click	Anyone	Couldn't find it — The product they want isn't showing up
Was browsing casually, left	Lapsing Customer (haven't visited in months)	Distraction — They'll probably come back
Step 7: The Right Intervention at the Right Moment (The Magic)
Now the system triggers a personalized intervention — the right message for the right person:

Same situation (abandoned a $1,299 TV), but different personas get different responses:

Persona	What They See
🏷️ Value Hunter	A sliding overlay: "🏷️ Price Drop Alert! Here's 10% off — expires in 2 hours!"
🚀 Tech Enthusiast	An overlay: "💎 Exclusive Member Price — members save 5% on this TV"
🔬 Researcher	An overlay: "📊 Expert Comparison Guide — see how this TV compares to top alternatives"
⭐ Loyalty User	An overlay: "⭐ VIP Price Unlocked — your loyalty gets you special pricing"
💤 Lapsing Customer	An email template: "We miss you! Here's 15% off to welcome you back"
💼 Business Buyer	An overlay: "💼 Business Volume Pricing — save up to 20% on bulk orders"
This is the "wow" moment in the demo — same product, same abandon action, but completely different interventions based on who the customer is.

Step 8: The Live Dashboard (What the Business Team Sees)
On a separate page (/dashboard), there's a real-time analytics dashboard that shows:

Persona Pie Chart: How many of each persona type are currently active
Live Event Feed: A scrolling list of every abandon event as it happens, showing which persona left, why, and what intervention fired
Intervention Log: A table of all interventions triggered
Revenue Recovery Tracker: How much money was "at risk" from abandonments, and how much the system estimates it recovered through interventions
Stats Cards: Total sessions, total abandons, interventions triggered, and estimated revenue saved
All of this updates in real-time — as customers browse and abandon on the storefront, the dashboard reflects it instantly.

Step 9: The Demo Simulator (Making It Look Alive)
There's a script (demo.sh) that simulates 20+ fake customers browsing the store simultaneously — different personas, different products, different abandon patterns. This makes the dashboard come alive with flowing data for the presentation.

🔌 How the Pieces Connect
You open the storefront (Next.js)
        │
        ├── Your browser creates a WebSocket connection to the backend
        │   (like an open phone line that stays connected)
        │
        ├── You browse, click, add to cart...
        │   Every action sends a message through the WebSocket
        │
        ▼
The backend (Python FastAPI) receives your actions
        │
        ├── Looks up your persona: "Ah, this is a Value Hunter"
        ├── Detects abandon: "They left the cart page with items!"
        ├── Classifies reason: "Value Hunter + cart abandon = price too high"
        ├── Decides intervention: "Show them a 10% discount overlay"
        │
        ├──► Sends intervention back to YOUR browser → overlay pops up!
        └──► Broadcasts to dashboard → event shows up in real-time feed

💰 Why This Matters for Best Buy
The business case is straightforward:

Best Buy gets ~15 million sessions per month where someone abandons
Today they send a generic "you left something in your cart" email 24 hours later
This system intervenes within seconds, with a personalized message
Even a 2% improvement in recovery rate = $21.6 million/month in extra revenue
That's over $250 million per year
The key insight: a generic reminder email treats all customers the same. This system treats each customer as the individual they are.
